We will quantify the difference of the Rydberg constant of hydrogen an deuterium.

Theory

Bohr model

When attempting to describe the movement of the electron in the atomic shell in a classical way, several problems occur:

  • When considering the photoelectric effect, the energy of the emitted electron doesn't depend on the intensity of the incoming light.
  • The radius of the orbit in which the electron circles the nucleus should be arbitrary, but the spectrum shows discrete lines, which means that the electron can only be located in discrete radii.
  • In the orbit the electron is permanently accelerated, thus radiating electromagnetic waves, leading to a loss of energy. After a short while, the electron should lose all its energy and as a consequence, no stationary atomic states should exist.
The Bohr model

To overcome these difficulties Nils Bohr proposed, in 1913, the Bohr postulates:

  1. Electrons orbit the nucleus. They are held in orbit by an electrostatic force.
  2. Electrons can only be in certain, permitted orbits and an electron does not emit radiation when it is in one of these orbits. Their radii are quantized - each energy state has a specific radius proportional to the angular momentum:
  3. An electron only emits radiation when it "falls" from a higher energy state to a lower state, and it can only absorb radiation by making a transition from a lower to a higher energy state:

In this experiment, this difference will be quantified.

Note:

  • The range of the splitting of the spectral lines induced by the isotope shift is of the order of the hyperfine structure (see chapter below), though in the case of hydrogen and deuterium it is big enough to be seen with our tools.
  • The isotope splitting is highly nontrivial to compute for systems with more than one electron because of the terms with mixed momentum operators of different electrons in the Hamiltonian.

Ebert monochromator

Ebert monochromator 1: Functional principle

An Ebert monochromator uses a diffraction grating to split up a beam in its spectral components. The functional principle is shown in the figure on the right.

The entrance and exit slits (S1 and S2) are located in the focal plane of the concave mirror M on the right. The incoming light is therefore reflected parallel to a rotatable diffraction grating M'. The light diffracted by the grating at the same angle will be reflected by M again and focused on S2. You will find this setup tuned.

However, one problem occurs which is explained in the figure on the left:

Ebert monochromator 2: Image defects

A point a at S1 is mapped onto a line at S2, which leads to image defects. Thus, the slits are shaped in a circular arc with the center C (located on the optical axis). So a point at S1 is still mapped onto a line, but only a point of this line will be transmitted, hence avoiding image defects. Experimentation

  1. Check the correct position of the table relative to the measurement tape (the distance between the mirror on top of the Ebert monochromator and the measurement tape will be needed for the analysis)
  2. Adjust the optical arrangement using the sodium lamp (be careful to deflect as much light as you can to the entrance slit S1. A condenser right behind the lamp might be helpful)
  3. Measure the spectral lines of the sodium and zinc lamp (it's crucial to take down the order of the observed maxima as well)
  4. Measure the Balmer series (hydrogen lamp). Especially in high order maxima, you will notice a splitting of the spectral lines. Why is that? Take down both lines.


Analysis

  1. Calibrate the measurement tape using the spectral lines of sodium and zinc and determine the monochromator constant . Compare your value to the geometrically computed one.
  2. Calculate the resolution of the apparatus using the sodium doublet.
  3. Calculate the Rydberg constant for hydrogen and deuterium.
