Motivation

Essential information about the physical behavior of crystals is given by the band model of solid state bodies. The electrons are arranged on specific energy bands which are separated by prohibited areas, the energy gaps. The energy gap is a characteristic value which divides solid bodies into conductor, semiconductor and isolator. The structure of the photon absorption spectrum gives information about the value of the energy gap of a crystal.

Aim of the experiment

The intention of the experiment is the measurement of the energy gap of the direct semiconductor gallium arsenide (GaAs) and the indirect semiconductor silicium (Si) at room temperature and at T=77K.

The measurement is realized with a monochromator to identify the absorption edges. Several measurements will be done, like the spectrum of the lamp or the calibration of the monochromator.

Crystallography

Various crystal lattices

Illustration of monocrystal, polycrystal and amorphous phases, Picture from reference 1

To classify solid state bodies on the basis of their sterically structures it has to distinguish between the following types

Mono-crystal: The places of the atoms can be described by a periodic lattice, which is in ideal case given in the whole crystal. The periodic lengths of this lattice are characteristic for the specific solid body.

Polycrystalline solid bodies: They exist of numerous small mono-crystals, which vary in size and orientation. There is no single lattice structure for the whole solid body.

Amorphous solid bodies: The atoms/molecules are discretionary irregular located and no specific arrangement can be found.

Liquid crystal: This is an intermediate state between crystalline solid body and liquid. Here the places of the atoms/molecules vary statistically. The state of a liquid crystal can be changed by the variation of its temperature or by an exterior field which creates periodicities.

In this experiment there is a focus on mono-crystals. The following observations are limited to atomic crystal lattices where each spot of the lattice is occupied by an atom. It is developed a periodic potential because each atom adds a coulomb potential where the electrons of the atoms are located.


Crystal structures of Si and GaAs

Silicon crystallizes in a diamond cubic crystal structure, with a lattice spacing of 5.43 Å. The crystal structure of the Gallium Arsenide is zinc blende with a lattice constant of a = 5.65 Å.

Band structures and transfer processes

Band structure of insulator, semiconductor and metal

There is a very simplified model for the theory of band structure. It gives a solid state body the characteristic of being an insulator, a semiconductor or a metal. The distance between the valence band and the conduction band and furthermore the location of the Fermi level are the essential parts of that characteristics. This is given in the illustrated figure. The Fermi energy is the maxiumum energy of the electrons.

Illustration of a metal, a semiconductor and an insulator band structure, Picture from reference 2

The energy band structure is composed of three parts: the valence band, the forbidden zone (or the bandgap ) and the conduction band. To raise electrons from the valence band to the conduction band a supply of energy is necessary. This has to be larger than the band distance . This energy can be given by a tension feeded exterior. If the Fermi level is placed within an energy band, then the energy states are occupied and the energy levels above are free. If the electrons are placed in the highest energy band, they can drift in direction of the electrical field by feeding an exterior voltage. Then current flows and the solid state body is a conductor.

A non-conductor or insulator has the characteristic that the Fermi level is located within the prohibited zone. The energy absorption of the electrons has to be higher than the band distance , therefore a very high exterior voltage would be necessary. In general this amount of voltage can not be realised. An insulator is not able to conduct because the conduction band is not occupied.

The opposite of an insulator is a metal. Here the valence and conduction band overlap an the electrons can get easily through the conduction band. The Fermi level is located in the conduction band. Here a current is able to flow because the places of the conduction band are partially occupied. All monovalent metals, like sodium (Na) or copper (Cu), are conductors.

The third type are semiconductors. The energygap has a size which can be vanquished by additional energy, the Fermi level is located in the forbidden band gap.

This simplified model does not describe the real bandstructures.

Absorption phenomena

The mechanism of absorption which gives an information about the value of the gap between the valence band and the conduction band is called the eigenabsorption. The light wave which is radiated through the crystal has the energy . If this energy is smaller than the energy difference of the maximum of the valence band and the minimum of the conduction band edge, the wave will pass through the crystal, except for a few reflective parts, in case no other mechanism of absorption happens. If the energy of the light wave is bigger than the energy gap, , the photon will be absorbed. The energy is given to an electron which can be excited from the valence band to the conduction band. Within this energy increase the electron has two possibilities of switching the bands, a direct or an indirect transfer.

Temperature dependence of the band gap

The band gap typically decreases with increasing temperature. The reasons for this are the change of electron–phonon interaction and the expansion of the lattice.

Doping and p-n junction

Certain impurities increase the electrical properties of semiconductors. The process of replacing atoms by some impurities is called doping. The addition of boron atoms to silicon in the proportion of 1 boron atom to 10 5 silicon atoms increases the conductivity of pure silicon by a factor of 10 3 at room temperature.

Impurity atoms that can give an electron are called donors. Impurity atoms that can accept an electron are called acceptors.


Exemple of doping for silicon: n and p-type silicon.

n-type Silicon: Silicon has 4 valence electrons and arsenic has 5 valence electrons. 4 electrons of the arsenic are used to form the covalent bonds, while the fifth electron is available for the conduction.

Electrons in the crystal

Electrons in a crystal can be located next to free atoms, in most cases then they are given in the interior energy shells. If the linkage is sufficiently weak electrons can move quite freely in the crystal because of the force of attraction according to the Coulomb potential of the neighboring atoms and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle in their own energy. These electrons are called delocalized. This phenomenon is characteristic for metals and is responsible for the electrical conductivity.


Electrons in the potential box

One-dimensional potential box

The simplest form of the particle in a box model considers a one-dimensional system. Here, the particle may only move backwards and forwards along a straight line with impenetrable barriers at either end. The walls of a one-dimensional box may be visualised as regions of space with an infinitely large potential energy. Conversely, the interior of the box has a constant, zero potential energy. This means that no forces act upon the particle inside the box and it can move freely in that region. However, infinitely large forces repel the particle if it touches the walls of the box, preventing it from escaping.

Security with liquid Nitrogen

Liquid nitrogen is a cryogenic. In this experiment it is used for cool the crystals to 77 Kelvins, its boiling temperature.

Atmospheric air is composed of 80% of nitrogen and 20% of oxygen. When the percentage of ogyxen decreases it is hazardous and can be mortal if the percentage of oxygen is lower than 6%. Air circulation in experiment rooms should be sufficient.

For the experiment day closed shoes and long pants are mandatory.

Gloves and glasses are mandatory for the liquid nitrogen manipulation.

Experimental set-up

The main components of the set-up are : the lamp, the monochromator, a photodiode and a multimeter. Some lenses are used for focussing the beam, the polarization filters, the colored filters and the cryostat.

Illustration of the experimental set-up

Halide lamp

The light of a 50 watt halide lamp (1) is focused by a converging lens to the entrance slit of a monochromator(2). The light intensity is quite constant in the needed spectral area. After the exit slit the light is bundled by a biconvex lense and is collimated to the crystal (which is located in the cryostat(3)). Then the light is again focused by some other lenses and reaches the photodiode(4). The photodiode is linked to a multimeter. The absorption coefficient can be calculated with the displayed diode voltage.

For measuring the light intensity without the crystals, the cryostat has to be removed. All parts of the experiment, except the cryostat, are assembled on an optical table. A shield against scatter light in the photodiode is a wooden box.


Monochromator

Illustration of the monochromator

The light of the lamp is focused to the entrance slit and is reflected by the mirror to the lattice (or grating). The diffracted light of the lattice is reflected by the mirror to the exit slit . For gaining different wavelengths the lattice can be turned by a regulating screw. Via that screw it is possible to get wavelengths of a huge spectrum with a linear relation to the position of the screw. All wavelengths can be resolved constantly well with the monochromator. The resolving capacity depends just on the used entrance and exit slit.

Crystals and cryostat

For the measurement of the absorption spectrum the crystals are arranged in the cryostat. For cooling the crystals to 77K liquid nitrogen is needed. First a vacuum is created by a membrane pump. The low pressure avoids condensation on the surface of the crystals at T=77K. If the measurement is done for a long space of time the windows of the cryostat can be clouded. To avoid this effect a blower is used to dry the windows. Gallium arsenide and silicium crystals are arranged in the same plasticcovered metalholder.

Photodiode

The detection of the incoming photons is realized by a photodiode. A photodiode is a semiconductor device that converts light into current. The current is generated when photons are absorbed in the photodiode.

The photodiode is composed of a p-n junction, more informations about doping and p-n junctions is available in the upper section (Doping and p-n junction). The p-n junction is made from a crystal separated in two regions. Here the depletion layer photo effect of a pn-junction is used. If there is no voltage, the electrons are located in the n-zone and the holes are located in the p-zone. The charge carriers have a constantly chaotic thermal movement. Next to the boundary layer arises recombination between n-zone and p-zone. The donors are located in the n-zone and the acceptors are located in the p-zone on static spots in the crystal. An electrical field is created, which avoids the diffusion of the charge carriers as long as there is no balanced state. In this state there is no voltage on the diode connections.

If the diode is irradiated by light which has a higher energy than the energy gap of the material, electrons are transferred into the conduction band. The new charge carriers move, accordingly to their charge, in the electrical field of the boundary layer. During this process free electrons reach the n-zone and positive holes reach the p-zone. The potential difference is changed and a photo voltage arises at the diode connections. If the connections are bypassed, a photo current flows. This current is proportional to the irradiated light intensity.

One polarisation filter is turned with an angle in comparison to the other. This angle can be varied . The polarisation filters lose their effect at a wavelength and the incoming light can not be absorbed completely anymore. A wavelength below 650 nm needs to be used for this part of the experiment.


2. Calibration of the monochromator

Calibration of the monochromator

To give a relation between the display of the monochromator and the outcoming wavelength, the monochromator has to be calibrated. This is done by three different colored filters (F) which are placed behind the monochromator. The wavelengths of the transmitted light of each filter are , and . The wavelength is varied by the monochromator as long as the specific wavelength of the colored filter is found. For each filter about 30 values have to be measured.


3. First Measurement of the total lamp spectrum

The spectrum to determine the absorption coefficient has to be measured. One has to bear in mind that the intensity is neither constant referring to the time nor to the wavelength. It is used the set-up seen in section „Experimental set-up“ but without the cryostat. The wavelength is varied by a micrometer screw of the monochromator. From 400 to 1200 nm, one measurement every 20 nm can be done.


4. Measurement of the absorption edge of GaAs and Si (room temperature)

With the set-up (seen in section „Experimental set-up“) the energy gap of both crystals can be measured. The cryostat is placed (without cooling) in the optical path. Its position can be proofed by holding a sheet of paper behind the cryostat and checking the intensity of the light beam. From 400 to 1200 nm, one measurement every 20 nm can be done. The interesting area has to be measured detailed.


5. Second Measurement of the lamp spectrum

To measure the temporal change of the intensity a second lamp spectrum is measured. From 400 to 1200 nm, one measurement every 20 nm can be done.

The students need to be careful and compare both lamp spectra (the first and the second), and look if the values are constant and similar.


6. Measurement of the absorption edge of GaAs and Si (T=77K)

To cool the cryostat to 77K, liquid nitrogen is necessary. The ventilator is turned on to avoid condensation on the windows. It has to be checked if there is enough nitrogen during the measurement. The opening is covered by a rag because fog disturbes the measurement. If the temperature is stable the measurement can be started. From 400 to 1200 nm, one measurement every 20 nm can be done. The interesting area has to be measured detailed.


7. Third Measurement of the total lamp spectrum

To measure the temporal change of the intensity a third lamp spectrum is measured. From 400 to 1200 nm, one measurement every 20 nm can be done.

The students need to be careful and compare all lamp spectra (the first, the second and the third), and look if the values are constant and similar.
